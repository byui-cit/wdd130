Activity Instructions

Estimated Time: 45 minutes

  1. Create the new HTML file

    For this assignment, choose one of the pages you identified last week and made wireframes for that you will complete as your sub-page. A sub-page is a page with links back and forth to your main page. You only need to code one sub-page for the rafting site.

    Want a shortcut? We know that many of the elements from our home page should also be on your child page: header, navigation, footer, colors, etc. So use the index.html as a starting point. Make a new file in your editor and save it with a meaningful name (for example if I were making a contact page I would name my new file contact.html) then copy and paste the code from your index.html into your new file, update the <title>, and delete the content that is specific to the home page and Voila! You have a great start on your new page.

    Note

    You do not want to create a new .css file for this new page. This could create a problem of having pages look different in important ways because the styles in one file were updated and not the other. You should only have one .css file for your entire site. Add any new styles for this new html page to the bottom of your existing .css.

  2. Add new content

    Add the html necessary in your new page to contain the new content.

    Remember that the content I have provided can be found in I-Learn or here: subpage content.

    Pay attention to your wireframe and try to make the HTML represent your layout, ie. create boxes (sections or divs) to represent the rows and columns of your layout and to group related content together. Open your website into your browser to make sure the web page displays correctly. Pay special attention to whether your images are all displaying. Once verified, begin adding the CSS to your site .css file to finish the page.

    Once you finish the first subpage, repeat the process for the second. Both pages are due with the final on the last week. To make sure you don't run out of time get one of your subpages done by the end of week 8.

Grading

This activity will be graded next week using the following rubric:

  • Significant textual content consisting of appropriate content for your audience so that they can understand the meaning and message of the page. There must also be at least one image in addition to the logo. You must have working links to your main page and any child pages (to and from) you are creating for this website. Your header (logo/navigation bar) and your footer (copyright/name and social media icon links) from the main page. Your page must be visually appealing. (20 pts)

What is meant by “visually appealing?”

I wish there was a non-subjective way to measure this, but there isn't. Generally you only lose points for this with things that are obvious. For example, text that is difficult to read because it uses a color to similar to the background, a navigation bar that is directly against a banner or the text of the page rather than having some space around it, use of a color scheme that obviously indicates no thought was put into the color choices (this doesn't mean that you can't use white - it's more stating that if you just threw colors onto the page for the sake of throwing colors onto the page and it is obvious, you'll lose points), etc.

If you simply copy and pasted the text from the content page in most cases it will not be visually appealing. Make sure that you format it to make it more readable! Be generous with <p> tags, padding, margins, and maybe even <ul>s. You will lose points on this if you do not put some thought into the readability of the text.

What is considered “significant content?”

Each page of a website should clearly convey to the user what the page is for. If it is a main or home page, it should provide the user with enough information to know what your site is about, whether it contains the information or products they are looking for, and, thereby, whether they should stick around and keep looking through your website. As another example, if you have a "Contact Us" page, you usually need more than just a contact form and email address. The page should have enough information that the user knows what the contact form and email address is for, what happens when they contact you, what type of information you want the user to provide when contacting you, etc.
